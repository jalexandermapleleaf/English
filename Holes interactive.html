            <h2>About the Book</h2>

            <p><em>Holes</em> (1998) is a Newbery Medal-winning novel by Louis Sachar, blending adventure, mystery, and family curses across three interwoven timelines. The story follows Stanley Yelnats, a boy wrongfully sent to Camp Green Lake—a brutal juvenile facility where boys dig 5-foot holes daily under the Texas sun. What seems like punishment hides a dark secret tied to Stanley’s family, a legendary outlaw, and a century-old curse.</p>

            <h2>Key Facts</h2>

            <ul>

                <li>Author: Louis Sachar (also known for <em>Sideways Stories from Wayside School</em>)</li>

                <li>Published: 1998</li>

                <li>Awards: Newbery Medal (1999), National Book Award for Young People’s Literature (1998)</li>

                <li>Genre: Adventure, Mystery, Realistic Fiction (with supernatural elements)</li>

                <li>Target Audience: Middle School (Grades 6–8)</li>

            </ul>

            <h2>Why It Matters</h2>

            <p><em>Holes</em> explores timeless themes like friendship, justice, and resilience while addressing real-world issues (prejudice, wrongful conviction). Its non-linear structure and memorable characters make it a favorite for teaching literary analysis—especially how past actions shape the present.</p>

        </div>

            <h2>Main Characters</h2>

            <div class="character-grid">

                <div class="character-card">

                    <div class="character-name">Stanley Yelnats IV</div>

                    <div class="character-role">Protagonist</div>

                    <p>A kind-hearted, overweight teen wrongfully convicted of stealing Clyde Livingston’s sneakers. Sent to Camp Green Lake, he evolves from a bullied outcast to a brave leader, forming a life-changing friendship with Zero.</p>

                    <p><strong>Key Traits:</strong> Loyal, resilient, selfless</p>

                    <p><strong>Family Curse:</strong> Descended from Elya Yelnats, who broke a promise to Madame Zeroni.</p>

                </div>

                <div class="character-card">

                    <div class="character-name">Zero (Hector Zeroni)</div>

                    <p>A quiet, misunderstood boy at Camp Green Lake. Initially labeled “stupid” (hence “Zero”), he’s secretly a math prodigy and Madame Zeroni’s descendant. He stole Clyde Livingston’s sneakers, leading to Stanley’s wrongful arrest.</p>

                    <p><strong>Key Traits:</strong> Intelligent, loyal, resilient</p>

                    <p><strong>Secret:</strong> His real name is Hector Zeroni—he’s been homeless since his mother abandoned him.</p>

                </div>

                <div class="character-card">

                    <div class="character-name">The Warden (Ms. Walker)</div>

                    <div class="character-role">Antagonist</div>

                    <p>The cruel, greedy owner of Camp Green Lake. She forces boys to dig holes to find Kissin’ Kate Barlow’s hidden treasure—her family’s lost fortune.</p>

                    <p><strong>Key Traits:</strong> Cruel, manipulative, obsessed</p>

                    <p><strong>Weapon:</strong> Rattlesnake venom nail polish (toxic when wet).</p>

                </div>

                <div class="character-card">

                    <div class="character-name">Kissin’ Kate Barlow</div>

                    <p>A legendary outlaw with a tragic past. Once a beloved schoolteacher (Katherine Barlow), she turned to crime after her lover (Sam, the onion man) was murdered by racists. She kissed the men she killed—hence her nickname.</p>

                    <p><strong>Key Traits:</strong> Vengeful, tragic, loyal</p>

                    <p><strong>Legacy:</strong> Buried a treasure that the Warden’s family has searched for generations.</p>

                </div>

                <div class="character-card">

                    <div class="character-name">Supporting Cast</div>

                    <ul>

                        <li><strong>Mr. Sir:</strong> A bitter counselor who torments Stanley (quits smoking, eats sunflower seeds).</li>

                        <li><strong>Mr. Pendanski (“Mom”):</strong> A hypocritical counselor who claims to care for boys but mocks Zero.</li>

                        <li><strong>X-Ray:</strong> Leader of Group D—shrewd and manipulative, he convinces Stanley to give him any “finds.”</li>

                        <li><strong>Elya Yelnats:</strong> Stanley’s great-great-grandfather, whose broken promise caused the family curse.</li>

                        <li><strong>Madame Zeroni:</strong> An old Egyptian woman who cursed Elya Yelnats—Zero’s great-great-grandmother.</li>

                    </ul>

            <h2>Character Relationships</h2>

            <button class="collapsible">Stanley & Zero</button>

            <div class="content">

                <p>Stanley and Zero’s friendship is the heart of the novel. They form a “trade”: Zero digs part of Stanley’s hole in exchange for reading lessons. Their bond transcends race, class, and social labels—Stanley carries Zero up “God’s Thumb” to break the curse, fulfilling Elya Yelnats’ promise to Madame Zeroni.</p>

            </div>

            <button class="collapsible">Elya Yelnats & Madame Zeroni</button>

            <div class="content">

                <p>Elya Yelnats fell in love with Myra Menke but couldn’t compete with Igor Barkov’s fat pig. Madame Zeroni gave him a piglet and taught him to carry it up a mountain to make it grow—on the condition that Elya carry her up the mountain afterward. Elya forgot his promise, leading to a curse: “You and your descendants will be doomed for all eternity.”</p>

            </div>

            <h2>Three Interwoven Timelines</h2>

            <p>The novel’s plot unfolds across three timelines, all connected by Camp Green Lake and the Yelnats family curse:</p>

            <h3>1. Stanley’s Time at Camp Green Lake (1990s)</h3>

            <div class="timeline">

                <div class="timeline-item">

                    <div class="timeline-date">Day 1</div>

                    <p>Stanley arrives at Camp Green Lake, learns he must dig 5-foot holes daily. Meets Group D (X-Ray, Zero, Armpit, etc.).</p>

                </div>

                <div class="timeline-item">

                    <div class="timeline-date">Weeks Later</div>

                    <p>Stanley finds a gold tube with “KB” (Kissin’ Kate Barlow) but gives it to X-Ray to stay in the group’s good graces.</p>

                </div>

                <div class="timeline-item">

                    <div class="timeline-date">Months Later</div>

                    <p>Stanley teaches Zero to read; Zero digs Stanley’s hole. The Warden bans the trade, and Zero runs away after hitting Mr. Pendanski.</p>

                </div>

                <div class="timeline-item">

                    <div class="timeline-date">Escape & Rescue</div>

                    <p>Stanley steals a truck to find Zero, crashes it, and tracks Zero to the “Mary Lou” boat. They climb “God’s Thumb” (a mountain), find water and onions, and break the curse.</p>

                </div>

                <div class="timeline-item">

                    <div class="timeline-date">Climax</div>

                    <p>Stanley and Zero return to Camp Green Lake, dig up Kate Barlow’s treasure, and are trapped by yellow-spotted lizards. They survive (onion blood repels lizards) and are rescued by Stanley’s lawyer.</p>

                </div>

                <div class="timeline-item">

                    <div class="timeline-date">Resolution</div>

                    <p>Camp Green Lake is shut down. Stanley’s family uses the treasure to buy a new home; Zero finds his mother with the help of private investigators.</p>

                </div>

            </div>

            <h3>2. Elya Yelnats’ Promise (1800s)</h3>

            <button class="collapsible">Elya’s Story</button>

            <div class="content">

                <p>Elya Yelnats, a Latvian teen, falls in love with Myra Menke but couldn’t compete with Igor Barkov’s fat pig. Madame Zeroni gave him a piglet and taught him to carry it up a mountain to make it grow—on the condition that Elya carry her up the mountain afterward. Elya forgets his promise, sails to America, and the curse begins. His descendants (including Stanley) suffer “bad luck” for generations.</p>

            </div>

            <h3>3. Kissin’ Kate Barlow’s Revenge (1900s)</h3>

            <button class="collapsible">Kate’s Story</button>

            <div class="content">

                <p>Katherine Barlow is a kind schoolteacher in Green Lake (then a thriving town). She falls in love with Sam, the Black onion man, but their kiss sparks a racist mob. Sam is murdered, the town’s lake dries up (a “divine punishment”), and Katherine becomes the outlaw “Kissin’ Kate Barlow.” She robs Stanley’s great-grandfather (Stanley Yelnats I) and buries her treasure—what the Warden is desperate to find.</p>

        <div class="tab-content" id="themes">

            <h2>Major Themes</h2>

            <button class="collapsible">Friendship & Loyalty</button>

            <div class="content">

                <p>Stanley and Zero’s friendship is the novel’s emotional core. They sacrifice for each other (Stanley carries Zero up the mountain; Zero digs Stanley’s hole) and prove that loyalty can overcome even the worst circumstances. Their bond transcends race, class, and social labels.</p>

            </div>

            <button class="collapsible">Justice & Injustice</button>

            <div class="content">

                <p>The novel critiques systemic injustice: Stanley’s wrongful conviction, Sam’s murder (due to racism), and the Warden’s exploitation of vulnerable boys. It asks: Who gets to define “justice” and who is left behind?</p>

            </div>

            <button class="collapsible">Fate vs. Free Will</button>

            <div class="content">

                <p>The Yelnats family curse suggests fate—but Stanley’s choice to help Zero proves free will can break cycles. Elya’s mistake (fate) is corrected by Stanley’s selflessness (free will), showing that we control our own redemption.</p>

            </div>

            <button class="collapsible">Resilience & Growth</button>

            <div class="content">

                <p>Camp Green Lake’s brutal conditions force Stanley to grow physically (he loses weight, gains strength) and emotionally (he stands up for himself and others). Zero, too, finds his voice and identity—proving resilience isn’t about being strong, but about keeping going.</p>

            </div>

            <h2>Key Symbols</h2>

            <button class="collapsible">Holes</button>

            <div class="content">

                <p>Literal: Punishment at Camp Green Lake. Metaphorical: “Holes” in the truth (secrets), “holes” in justice (wrongful conviction), and “holes” in the past (unresolved trauma). Digging holes forces boys to confront the past—both the camp’s and their own.</p>

            </div>

            <button class="collapsible">Onions</button>

            <div class="content">

                <p>Symbolize life, healing, and protection. Sam’s onions cure illness, repel yellow-spotted lizards, and sustain Stanley and Zero on God’s Thumb. They represent the power of nature and the “good” that persists despite injustice.</p>

            </div>

            <button class="collapsible">Yellow-Spotted Lizards</button>

            <div class="content">

                <p>Represent death and the consequences of greed. They live in holes (hiding from the sun) and are immune to most threats—except onion blood. Their presence reminds characters (and readers) that greed and cruelty lead to destruction.</p>

            </div>

            <button class="collapsible">God’s Thumb</button>

            <div class="content">

                <p>A mountain peak resembling a thumb—Stanley’s great-grandfather’s refuge after Kate Barlow robbed him. It symbolizes hope, redemption, and the fulfillment of promises. Climbing it breaks the curse, as Stanley carries Zero (Madame Zeroni’s descendant) to safety.</p>

            </div>

        </div>

        <div class="tab-content" id="rhetorical">

            <h2>Rhetorical Devices in <em>Holes</em></h2>

            <p>Louis Sachar uses a variety of rhetorical and literary devices to enhance the novel’s meaning, tone, and engagement. Below are the key devices with examples from the text:</p>

            <div class="device-card">

                <div class="device-name">1. Irony (Situational, Verbal, Dramatic)</div>

                <p><strong>Definition:</strong> A contrast between expectation and reality.</p>

                <p><strong>Examples:</strong></p>

                <ul>

                    <li><strong>Situational:</strong> Camp Green Lake has no lake (it’s a desert); the “character-building” holes are actually a treasure hunt; the yellow-spotted lizards (deadly) don’t bite Stanley and Zero (onion blood repels them).</li>

                    <li><strong>Verbal:</strong> Mr. Pendanski calls himself “Mom” but is cruel and dismissive of the boys.</li>

                    <li><strong>Dramatic:</strong> Readers learn Zero is Madame Zeroni’s descendant before Stanley does (we know he’s the key to breaking the curse).</li>

                </ul>

            </div>

            <div class="device-card">

                <div class="device-name">2. Foreshadowing</div>

                <p><strong>Definition:</strong> Hints or clues about future events in the story.</p>

                <p><strong>Examples:</strong></p>

                <ul>

                    <li>Stanley’s father’s work on a foot odor cure foreshadows the final “Sploosh” cure (made from Kate Barlow’s spiced peaches).</li>

                    <li>References to “God’s Thumb” early in the novel foreshadow Stanley and Zero’s refuge there.</li>

                    <li>Sam’s onions being called “healing onions” foreshadows they repel yellow-spotted lizards.</li>

                </ul>

            </div>

            <div class="device-card">

                <div class="device-name">3. Symbolism</div>

                <p><strong>Definition:</strong> Objects or actions that represent abstract ideas (beyond their literal meaning).</p>

                <p><strong>Examples:</strong> (See the <em>Themes & Symbols</em> tab for full details)</p>

                <ul>

                    <li>Holes = punishment, secrets, and unresolved trauma.</li>

                    <li>Onions = life, healing, and Sam’s legacy.</li>

                    <li>Sneakers = fate and injustice (Zero’s theft leads to Stanley’s arrest).</li>

                </ul>

            </div>

            <div class="device-card">

                <div class="device-name">4. Juxtaposition</div>

                <p><strong>Definition:</strong> Placing two ideas, characters, or settings side by side to highlight contrast.</p>

                <p><strong>Examples:</strong></p>

                <ul>

                    <li>1900s Green Lake (thriving, lush town) vs. 1990s Camp Green Lake (barren desert) – highlights the cost of racism and injustice.</li>

                    <li>Stanley (overweight, passive) vs. Zero (small, quiet but strong-willed) – their differences complement each other and strengthen their friendship.</li>

                    <li>The Warden’s polished appearance (nail polish, fancy cabin) vs. her cruel actions (exploiting children, using venom) – highlights her moral decay.</li>

                </ul>

            </div>

            <div class="device-card">

                <div class="device-name">5. Repetition</div>

                <p><strong>Definition:</strong> Repeating words, phrases, or ideas to emphasize a point.</p>

                <p><strong>Examples:</strong></p>

                <ul>

                    <li>The phrase “I’m not a thief” (Stanley repeats this to himself, emphasizing his innocence).</li>

                    <li>The lullaby “If only, if only” (sung by Stanley’s father, Elya Yelnats, and Zero’s mother – links generations and emphasizes longing).</li>

                    <li>The word “holes” (repeated throughout the novel, reinforcing its symbolic importance).</li>

                </ul>

            </div>

            <div class="device-card">

                <div class="device-name">6. Palindrome (Stanley Yelnats)</div>

                <p><strong>Definition:</strong> A word/phrase that reads the same backward and forward.</p>

                <p><strong>Example:</strong> “Stanley Yelnats” is a palindrome (spelled backward it’s “stanleY stanleY” – the last name Yelnats is Stanley reversed). This symbolizes the cyclical nature of fate and redemption (the curse is broken by a Yelnats helping a Zeroni, completing the cycle started by Elya).</p>

            </div>

            <div class="device-card">

                <div class="device-name">7. Flashback</div>

                <p><strong>Definition:</strong> Shifts to a past event to provide context or depth.</p>

                <p><strong>Examples:</strong> The flashbacks to Elya Yelnats’ promise (1800s) and Kate Barlow’s story (1900s) explain the curse and the camp’s purpose, connecting the past to the present.</p>

            </div>

        </div>
