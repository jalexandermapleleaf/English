📚 Vocabulary & Literary Terms

Study Reference | 学习参考

Review these key terms before starting the daily lessons. Use this section as a reference throughout the week.

Vocabulary (词汇)

Acknowledge

(verb) 承认；认可
To accept or recognize something (a fact, feeling, or truth).
🇨🇳 Chinese Context: In Chinese culture, when a teacher corrects you in class, you should acknowledge your mistake by saying "对不起，老师" (sorry, teacher). In Western culture, you might acknowledge it by saying "Thank you for the correction."

✓ Examples

  • The student acknowledged her mistake on the test
  • The company acknowledged the workers' hard effort during Spring Festival

Authentic

(adjective) 真实的；正宗的
Genuine; real; not fake or copied.
🇨🇳 Chinese Context: When you visit a restaurant in Chengdu, you want authentic Sichuan food (正宗川菜), not a fake version. Similarly, when buying a smartphone, you want an authentic iPhone, not a counterfeit copy.

✓ Examples

  • Authentic Beijing roast duck from Quanjude restaurant
  • Her authentic smile showed she was truly happy

Benefit

(noun) 好处；益处
An advantage; something that is helpful or positive.

✓ Examples

  • A benefit of living in Shanghai is access to international opportunities
  • Learning English has the benefit of opening career doors

Obstacle

(noun) 障碍；阻碍
Something that gets in the way and keeps you from making progress.

✓ Examples

  • Language barriers are a common obstacle for Chinese students studying abroad
  • Fear of making mistakes can be an obstacle to speaking English fluently

Philosophy

(noun) 哲学；人生观
A belief system or way of thinking about the world, life, or a particular topic.

✓ Examples

  • Jack Ma's business philosophy: "customers first, employees second"
  • A teacher's philosophy: "every student can succeed with the right support"

Stereotype

(noun) 刻板印象
A fixed, oversimplified idea about a type of person or thing.

✓ Examples (of stereotypes - these are WRONG)

  • "All Asian students are quiet in class" (not true for everyone)
  • "Girls are bad at science" (a harmful stereotype)

Literary Terms (文学术语)

Tension

(noun) 紧张感
A state of emotional strain or suspense created by conflict between characters or forces.

✓ Examples

  • The tension before exam scores are posted on the bulletin board
  • A basketball game tied in the final seconds creates tension

Mood

(noun) 氛围；情绪
The emotional atmosphere or feeling that a reader experiences while reading a text.

✓ Examples

  • A dark, foggy scene in a cemetery creates a creepy mood
  • A description of red lanterns creates a celebratory mood

Tone

(noun) 语气；口吻
The author's attitude or manner of expression toward the subject matter and audience.

✓ Examples

  • Sarcastic tone: "Oh great, another three-hour exam. How wonderful."
  • Encouraging tone: "You can do this! Keep trying and you'll succeed!"

Conflict

(noun) 冲突；矛盾
A struggle or disagreement between opposing forces, characters, or ideas that drives the plot forward.

✓ Examples

  • Person vs. Person: A student arguing with a teacher about a grade
  • Person vs. Self: Deciding between studying abroad or staying in China

Diction

(noun) 用词；措辞
The specific word choice made by an author to convey meaning, create tone, and shape the reader's interpretation.

✓ Examples

  • "The student walked to school" vs "The student trudged to school" (trudged shows tiredness)
  • "She was thin" vs "She was slender" vs "She was gaunt" (different connotations)

Focus: Text Evidence | 文本证据

What is Text Evidence? (什么是文本证据？)

Text evidence includes facts, data, or information from a text that helps support or prove an idea. It's like showing your work in math class - you prove your answer is correct by showing the evidence from the text.

Passage 1: Lang Lang's Piano Journey

When Lang Lang was nine, his piano teacher told him he had no talent. However, Lang Lang practiced eight hours every day, even on holidays. His fingers would sometimes bleed from the intense practice sessions, but he never complained. He would wake up at 5 AM to practice before school. At age 17, he performed with the Chicago Symphony Orchestra. Today, he is one of the world's most famous pianists. He often says that his success came not from natural talent, but from working harder than anyone else.

Practice Question 1: Which evidence BEST supports the idea that Lang Lang's success came from hard work rather than natural talent?

Rationale (解释)

✓ Correct! Option B shows specific hard work: "practiced eight hours every day, even on holidays." This concrete evidence demonstrates dedication. The other options either mention talent (A), just show results (C, D), but don't prove hard work.

Passage 2: The Terracotta Army Discovery

In March 1974, farmers in Xi'an were digging a well during a severe drought. They were not looking for historical treasures; they were just trying to find water for their crops. As they dug deeper, their shovels hit something hard. At first, they thought it was just a rock. But when they cleared away soil, they saw a clay face staring up at them. They had discovered the Terracotta Army. This discovery was completely accidental - they were looking for water, not treasures.

Practice Question 2: Which evidence shows the Terracotta Army discovery was accidental?

Rationale (解释)

✓ Correct! Option A directly states they "were not looking for historical treasures; they were just trying to find water." This proves the discovery was accidental - they had a different purpose (finding water) and found the army by chance.

Passage 3: Tu Youyou's Malaria Research

In the 1960s, Tu Youyou was assigned to find a cure for malaria. She reviewed over 2,000 traditional Chinese medicine recipes. After testing more than 380 herbal extracts without success, she found a reference to sweet wormwood (青蒿). Her initial experiments failed, but she tried a different approach: extracting at lower temperatures. This worked. Without hesitation, Tu Youyou volunteered to be the first human test subject, taking the extract herself to check for side effects. Her discovery has saved millions of lives.

Practice Question 3: Which evidence BEST shows Tu Youyou's dedication to finding a cure?

Rationale (解释)

✓ Correct! Option A provides specific numbers (2,000 recipes, 380 extracts) showing the massive amount of work she did. This concrete evidence proves her dedication. The other options mention results (B), context (C), or assignment (D) but don't show her personal effort.

📅 Wednesday In Class

Focus: Making Inferences | 推理判断

What is an Inference? (什么是推理？)

An inference is like being a detective. You use clues from the text (evidence) + what you already know (background knowledge) = a logical conclusion.

Passage 1: The Returned Exam

Chen Wei sat at his desk, staring at the math exam his teacher had just returned. He crumpled the paper tightly in his fist, then quickly shoved it deep into his backpack without looking at the score. His face turned red, and he kept his eyes fixed on his desk. When the bell rang, he was the first person out the door, nearly running to leave. His friends called out to him, asking how he did, but he pretended not to hear them.

Practice Question 4: What can you infer about Chen Wei's exam score?

Rationale (解释)

✓ Correct! Evidence: crumpling paper, red face, running away, avoiding friends.
Background knowledge: People act this way when embarrassed about bad news.
Inference: He probably did poorly and feels embarrassed.

Passage 2: The Art Class Secret

Xiaoming stays in the art room for two hours every Monday. His parents think he's at study hall. He brings an extra change of clothes but leaves them in his locker. Last week, his mother noticed paint stains on his fingers that he tried to hide. When she asked, he said he touched something painted on the subway. This Monday, his art teacher submitted one of Xiaoming's paintings to a national competition without telling him.

Practice Question 5: What can you infer about why Xiaoming keeps his art class secret?

Rationale (解释)

✓ Correct! Evidence: Parents think he's at study hall (not art), he hides paint stains, lies about them.
Background knowledge: Students hide activities when they think parents won't approve.
Inference: His parents don't approve of art class.

Passage 3: The Community Garden

Mr. Zhang, 76, tends a small garden plot with incredible care. His plot is only 3 square meters but bursting with vegetables. Every Thursday, he rides the bus across the city to his daughter's apartment and leaves fresh vegetables by her door. He never rings the doorbell or waits for her. His daughter is a busy executive who works 70-hour weeks and rarely visits him.

Practice Question 6: What can you infer about Mr. Zhang's relationship with his daughter?

Rationale (解释)

✓ Correct! Evidence: Tends garden with "incredible care," travels across city, leaves vegetables without waiting, daughter rarely visits.
Background knowledge: Parents show love through actions when distance exists.
Inference: He uses gardening to show love despite their distance.
